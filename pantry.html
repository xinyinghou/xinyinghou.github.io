Background

This is an indepedent study research project in McLearn Lab, CMU.

Last fall, we implemented a classroom study with a focus of finding the enjoyment-learning-balance in digital learning games. Based on the data collected, we did post hoc data analysis and understood deeper about how to design digital learning games to make it optimize student learning while retaining its core value as a playful environment.


This spring, with the goal of helping students using hints and error message more effectively, we applied both classroom setting and remote setting study in 6 middle schools.

We are currently analysising students’ log data and learning outcome data to see how they interact with these information and support.

Main Research Findings

Learning-Enjoyment Balance
RQ1: Do game mechanics that promote learning and those that promote enjoyment have different effects on students’ experience?

Enjoyment Condition (EC) students played the least number of rounds but had higher learning efficiency than the Control Condition (CC) students.
Two distinct gameplay patterns:
Learning Condition -> Repeated practice; Enjoyment Condition -> Exploration

RQ2: Do male students or female students learn from or enjoy games more?

Females gained more decimal knowledge than males in the posttest and delayed posttest across all conditions.

Students’ Learning Behavior Assessment
RQ1: Did students learn enough or need more practice at the end of their gameplay?

Students had the most difficulty with mastering the number line and sorting skills.
Only 26/159 students mastered all 5 skills, students tended to overpractice the skills they had previously mastered.

RQ2: What are the best feature sets to predict test scores and self-reported enjoyment?

Pretest score, Sorting mastery and Bucket mastery are in both posttest and delayed posttest models.
Enjoyment models did not perform as well as learning models

Open Learner Model and Open Enjoyment Model
RQ1: How does optimizing for enjoyment impact learning, and vice versa?

Following the open learner model led to better in-game learning and test performance.
Following the open enjoyment model was not associated with learning.

RQ2: Is there any benefit to opening up these models for the player’s inspection?

No relationship between following the open learner or enjoyment model and post-game enjoyment.

Project Highlights

01. Females learned more than males from the game across all conditions.

02. The open learner model caused students to replay more rounds of the completed mini-games.

03. Few students mastered all five skills in the game, while over-practice was very common.

04. Following the open learner model leads to higher in-game learning and test performance.

05. Students tended to provide maximum ratings to all mini-games, making the open enjoyment model not as effective.
